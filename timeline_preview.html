My Path

I didn’t take the straight road into geospatial. I came to it through consulting, a career reinvention, a global pandemic, a book I almost published, and a nonprofit I couldn’t not start. Here’s the story — and the moments that shaped how I build.

  1. 2012 – 2015 Where it started

    Consulting & research, Amman

    My career began far from code. I worked in management consulting and technology market research in Amman, Jordan — feasibility studies, quality systems, and TMT research for governments and multinationals. I learned how large institutions actually work: their processes, their politics, and the gap between how things are supposed to run and how they really do. That understanding still shapes everything I build today.

  2. 2018 – 2019 The pivot

    Teaching myself to code

    After relocating to the United States, I made the decision that changed everything: I taught myself to program. It started in a Georgetown coffee shop in Washington, DC, with the R language, a laptop, and a lot of stubbornness. No bootcamp, no CS degree — just curiosity and a conviction that the future belonged to people who could work fluently with data.

  3. 2019 – 2020 Foundations

    Business Intelligence at UC San Diego

    I enrolled in the Business Intelligence program at UC San Diego to formalize what I’d been teaching myself — relational databases, statistics, and the discipline of turning raw data into decisions. It was meant to be a bridge into IT service management. Life had other plans.

  4. 2020 The plot twist

    ServiceNow certified — then the world closed

    I completed my ServiceNow training and earned my CSA and ITSM certifications, ready to step into enterprise IT. Then COVID-19 hit. The pathway I’d planned disappeared overnight, and like millions of others I found myself out of work and locked down at home. It turned out to be one of the most productive periods of my life.

  5. 2020 First build

    My first real project

    Stuck at home, I went all in. My first real project was a bag-of-words text analysis — I learned the technique, replicated it, and produced a research-grade paper I was genuinely proud of. It was rough around the edges, but it was mine, and it proved to me I could actually do this.

  6. 2020 – 2021 The breakthrough

    A mentor, a thousand hours, and a first AI role

    Then I met a mentor — a postdoc AI researcher — who took me under his wing. I spent the lockdown devouring Python, data science, and machine learning on DataCamp, course after course. That mentorship led to my first industry role: an AI/NLP internship where I built a production natural language processing pipeline. I was officially a practitioner.

  7. 2021 The deep dive

    Writing a book on AI at Georgetown

    In 2021 I joined a book-writing program at Georgetown with Eric Koester. I chose to write about artificial intelligence — and threw myself in, interviewing more than 40 AI pioneers, researchers, and leaders. I got well past the halfway mark toward publication. The research transformed how I understood where technology and society were heading.

  8. 2021 The mission

    Founding AI Research Corps

    One story from my research stuck with me: in 2017, China watched AlphaGo defeat the world’s best Go player and declared a national AI strategy. The U.S. responded in 2018. I realized I was watching a civilizational shift — and that public institutions were being left behind, unable to keep pace with technology increasingly built on open source.

    So I paused the book and founded AI Research Corps, a 501(c)(3) nonprofit dedicated to closing that gap: bringing AI, open data, and modern geospatial tools to the public-sector institutions that need them most.

    AI Research Corps
  9. 2022 – 2023 Finding my field

    Geographic Information Systems

    After business intelligence, IT service management, data science, machine learning, NLP, and writing — I found the discipline that tied it all together: GIS. I spent a year and a half at UC San Diego immersed in geospatial science, and it was some of the most exciting learning of my life. Maps, spatial data, and location intelligence became the lens that made everything else make sense.

  10. 2023 – 2026 Into the field

    GIS & data lead, City of San Diego

    Soon after graduating, I became the GIS and data lead for one of the largest municipal operations on the West Coast — 225,000+ properties, a million-plus assets, 700+ routes. I finally got to do the thing I’d spent years preparing for: building real geospatial systems that move a city. I built RouteView, automated routing and reporting workflows, integrated enterprise platforms, and served as a technical lead on major policy transformations.

    Open RouteView More projects

    GIS Day 2024

    I started sharing what I was building with the wider community — presenting at GIS Day 2024 on how geospatial technology transforms government operations.

  12. 2025 Giving back

    Solid waste GIS — ASPRS / SDSU

    I presented to the ASPRS student chapter at San Diego State University on solid-waste GIS and the operational intelligence work I’d been doing — sharing how spatial data drives real decisions in a large municipal operation.

    Watch the talk
  13. 2026 Teaching

    Geospatial data science, in the open

    I published a long-form, interactive geospatial data science article — a map-driven case study you can scroll through — and kept presenting on cloud-native geospatial work and where the field is heading.

    Read the article
  14. 2026 Open source

    opentrash — bringing the tools into the open

    I began publishing opentrash, an open-source, cloud-native Python package for residential waste-collection analytics — taking the kind of tooling I’d built inside government and putting it in the open, where anyone can use and build on it.

    GitHub Repos
  15. Now What’s next

    Cloud-native open geospatial data

    Today I’m building cloud-native open geospatial data infrastructure — a registry that indexes 26,000+ datasets using STAC, GeoParquet, and DuckDB Spatial — and contributing to the OGC and the cloud-native geospatial community. The mission that started in a coffee shop continues: make powerful geospatial tools open, interoperable, and within reach for everyone.
